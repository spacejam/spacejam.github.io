cooperation-enabling mediation mechanisms

Groups that enable members to work toward desirable futures, both at the individual level and at other scales of social organization. Regardless of how crappy the place is currently, by supporting collective forward progress we can increase trust in each other over time. Trust is wiped out when the faith in a positive shared future as a collective entity is shaken.

Because trust is a function of future expectations, the perceived direction of change is critical in determining the ability to collectively act to actually meet challenges. Positive expectations more frequently lead to higher-quality collaborations. Negative expectations can fragment a society into smaller subgroups who feel like they are drowning and will push anyone else down that they can reach in order to keep breathing air.

We use various structures to mediate the vast and ever-shifting "conflict space" of conflicting human needs. Our trust in our society is heavily dependent on our perceived ability to drive the in-place conflict-shaping structures in a direction that is up to the task of mediating experienced conflict. Courts, laws, unions, telegram groups, etc... We have a wide variety of structures and we are constantly inventing new ones that can be wielded by various societal granularities.

But when these mechanisms fail to effectively mediate conflicts, and successive attempts to instate mediating structures are met with powerful resistance, it causes the trust that forms the core of any functioning society to rot away. Relationships that were once mediated through more or less consensual, if not ideal, acts of cooperation give way to more coercive means of domination.

The Universe of Obligation

The Universe of Obligation is a phrase coined by sociologist Helen Fein, who describes it as the people "Toward whom obligations are owed, to whom rules apply, and whose injuries call for amends."

Bretton Woods, the Meidner Plan, and Vietnam War Debts

Toward the end of WWII, the major economic powers of the world gathered in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire to discuss a system of international financial cooperation aiming to prevent financial that exacerbated foreign relations in the interwar period. The Bretton Woods System tied the major economic powers to the US dollar at fixed exchange rates that were permitted to fluctuate by up to 1% each year, along with a system of capital controls that reduced the risk of capital flight, tax evasion, speculative attacks and deindustrialization.

As a result of these capital controls, models such as The Meidner Plan became feasible, although never fully implemented. Variations of the plan were proposed by both Bernie Sanders and Elizabeth Warren during the Democratic primary leading up to the 2020 presidential election.

I see two classes of globally-expanding conflict that are insufficiently mediated by our current mix of structures:

  • 1. inter-generational conflict between young and old
  • 2. inter-class conflict between rich and poor

The old are preventing the young from working toward desirable futures. The rich are preventing the poor from working toward desirable futures. There is significant overlap, but not enough to treat these as a single issue. These are strong claims and they will be expanded upon below.

Society is decohering into tribes who have lost faith in the value of working together. Despite metrics claiming global extreme poverty to be at historically low rates, class and generational relations within developed countries are deteriorating.

summary of "Why Technology and Markets Alone Can't Avoid Overshoot" from Limits to Growth: The 30-Year Update

I am not a techno optimist. The rate at which pessimistic models are being invalidated by measured data is extremely concerning to me, because it is clear that our ability to guess how bad things will get is failing to account for some number of critical omitted variables. The book "limits to growth: 30-year update" by the celebrated systems theorist donella meadows has a section titled "why technology and markets alone can't avoid overshot" that I believe has a number of compelling reasons why I believe we need a more strongly course correcting action than what entrepreneurs are going to be able to realistically address. I believe a lot of people are better off now, and that by several compelling metrics we are enjoying less (physical) violence right now than before, but I believe this is being primarily driven by rapidly depleting an unknown number of critical resources that support medium term growth but constitute an externality on future generations where we are restricting their available decisions heavily to support systems that would otherwise lose profitability.

Everyday people who have been losing stability bit by bit since the 70s. Profits have grown in this time, but real wages have declined.

choice of models

Neoclassical econ (NCE hereafter) says wages come from marginal value added by the last employee, being intentionally "depoliticized" in the early 1900s to be more competitive for departmental funding by attempting to be as quantitative and Positive as physics, but anyone who has run a business knows that there are simple trade-offs between profits and wages, mediated by political employee actions. I think the NCE models work well for their purposes, but their purposes have nothing to do with helping humans generally meet their expectations and achieve long term goals that they want to believe in. There are other models that allow for reasoning about these political aspects of normal life, and they are extensively backed by empirical evidence. This is why I find Shaikh, sraffa etc useful - the fact that they do not apply to the same problems as NCE due to being intentionally open to modeling more political realities (while fitting real data far better at the firm level and lower) is exactly why they are interesting to me
